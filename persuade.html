Executive Summary

The Argument

This presentation synthesizes Gene Youngblood's cybernetic "expanded cinema" with Ian Bogost's "procedural rhetoric" to demonstrate that media's primary persuasive power lies not in representation but in regulation. Through live audience interaction and six concrete case studies, we trace how rules shape behavior, belief, and ultimately, reality.

The Stakes

From SimCity's urban planning assumptions to TikTok's attention economy, from credit scoring algorithms to AI-generated films, we live in a world where procedural arguments structure our choices before we're aware of them. The question isn't whether rules will regulate us—it's who gets to write them.

Bogost in One Move: Procedural Rhetoric

"The art of persuasion through rule-based representations and interactions—not words, images, or film."

— Ian Bogost, Persuasive Games (2007) [Source]

🎮
📊
🧠

Key Insight

A game doesn't tell you urban policy—it makes you discover that more police stations reduce crime. The rules do the arguing. You don't watch the argument. You live it. This shifts media studies from "what does this mean?" to "what does this let me do—and what does that do to me?"

Youngblood in One Move: Expanded Cinema

"The viewer feeds back into the environment, expanding creative potential. Media as cybernetic system, not screen."

— Gene Youngblood, Expanded Cinema (1970) [Source]

Traditional Cinema

Projection you receive

Expanded Cinema

Participation you co-create

Cybernetics 101

Youngblood saw cinema not as projection you receive but as participation you co-create. The viewer "re-creates discovery along with the artist, feeding back into the environment." Cybernetics 101: behavior shapes system, system shapes behavior. Youngblood's "expanded cinema" was already procedural—rules generating meaning through interaction.

How It Works

Name it: The Invisible Argument Loop. Rules create what you can do. That shapes what you do. Your actions feed back, reinforcing or challenging the system. Eventually: belief. Not "I was told this." "I experienced this."

"That's why rules beat images. Images ask you to agree. Rules make you complicit."

Case Studies: Rules in Action

🏙️

SimCity

Urban planning as procedural argument

The Rules

  • • Police stations reduce crime within fixed radius
  • • Zoning enforces strict residential/commercial separation
  • • Tax rates affect growth but not inequality

The Procedural Argument

"Rule: Police stations reduce crime. Behavior: You build more police. Belief: Crime = lack of enforcement."

Flip one rule:

What if crime responded to education funding? Same city, different ideology.

"SimCity's rules argue. Crime spikes? The solution is in the rules: place police. Not housing, not jobs, not history. The game doesn't show you 'tough on crime'—it makes you enact it."
💧

Darfur is Dying

The procedural argument against heroism

The Rules

  • • You play a powerless Darfuri child, not a hero
  • • Water-fetching missions fail frequently
  • • No "win" condition or heroic narrative arc

The Procedural Argument

"Rule: You play a powerless child. Behavior: You fail, repeatedly. Belief: Complexity resists simple solutions."

Flip one rule:

A "save everyone" button would convert systemic tragedy into individual triumph.

"The rules refuse heroism. The procedural argument: humanitarian crises aren't solved by awareness alone. The rules protect the argument from feel-good bullshit."
⏱️

TikTok FYP

The attention economy's procedural logic

The Rules

  • • Watch time = distribution
  • • Engagement velocity drives algorithm
  • • Trending topics get exponential amplification

The Procedural Argument

"Rule: Watch time = distribution. Behavior: Creators optimize for hook + outrage. Belief: The world is constantly breaking."

Flip one rule:

Optimize for "time well spent" instead. Same app, different nervous system.

"The For You Page doesn't show you what's true. It shows you what stops your thumb. The rule produces behavior: shorter videos, louder emotions, faster cuts."
💳

Credit Scoring Algorithms

Financial virtue as procedural assessment

The Rules

  • • Past behavior predicts future risk
  • • Traditional credit history is primary factor
  • • Medical debt penalizes even when paid

The Procedural Argument

"Rule: Past behavior predicts future risk. Behavior: You perform creditworthiness. Belief: Financial virtue is measurable and deserved."

Flip one rule:

Include gig economy cash-flow data. Same score, different futures distributed.

"The FICO algorithm—rules from the 1990s—rewards traditional credit, which white Americans access more easily. The procedural argument: your financial past determines your future. Not systemic. Personal."

AI Bridge: Procedural Authorship

Sunspring (2016)

AI trained on sci-fi scripts writes Sunspring. The rules: patterns, not meaning. Result: uncanny, accidental poetry.

Ars Technica | Academic Analysis

🔄

Pattern Recognition

LSTM neural network trained on thousands of sci-fi screenplays

📝

Procedural Generation

Generates dialogue based on statistical patterns, not narrative understanding

🎭

Human Interpretation

Actors perform the nonsense earnestly, creating meaning through performance

The Procedural Residue

The AI doesn't understand. It follows rules: statistical patterns of dialogue, scene structure, character arcs. The actors performed the nonsense earnestly—and something emerged. Not authorial intent. Procedural residue. The rules wrote a film. The argument: when media is authored by procedure, meaning becomes an accident of constraints.

Discussion Questions

❓

Interpretive

"What rules governed your last hour of media use? Not what you saw—what were you allowed to do, and what did that permission shape?"

Operationalizes the procedural framework for personal experience; forces attention from content to structure.

⚖️

Ethical

"SimCity argues that police reduce crime; Darfur is Dying argues that heroism fails. Both use rules to persuade. Is one more honest? What would 'honest rules' look like in a platform you use daily?"

Introduces normative evaluation without presupposing answers; demands translation to familiar systems.

🔧

Design/Governance

"If you had to rewrite one rule in TikTok's algorithm, or FICO's scoring model, or ChatGPT's moderation policy—what would you change, who would you need to convince, and what unintended behaviors might your new rule produce?"

Converts analysis to design practice; insists on systemic thinking (stakeholders, consequences, feedback).
